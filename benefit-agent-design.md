## Problem Statement

People have access to thousands of dollars in benefits, insurance payouts, reimbursements, and perks across employer plans, supplemental insurance, credit cards, memberships, and government programs. They don't miss these because they don't care — they miss them because they find out too late. The benefit was always there; the window has just already closed. Insurance companies' business models are built on the assumption (proven accurate so far) that a small percentage of policyholders will be organized enough to claim.

The Benefit Agent solves the timing problem: it tells you about your entitlements before the deadline passes, not after.

## Demand Evidence

**Personal:** Founder hit her head, had a $500 concussion benefit in her accident insurance policy, and didn't know to claim it before or after the incident. The medical treatment was covered separately; this was a distinct payout she simply didn't know existed or remembered to file.

**Social proof mechanism:** Every person she told had their own version of this story — a benefit discovered after the window closed, anger followed by helplessness. The reaction is not "that's interesting" — it's "that happened to me." That's real demand, not aspiration.

**Structural:** Behavioral economics literature confirms the mechanism — cognitive overload, procrastination, transaction cost vs. perceived benefit. This is documented underutilization, not a niche complaint.

**The business model insight:** Insurance companies offer these perks because only a small percentage will be organized enough to claim them. This isn't a bug in the system — it IS the system. That makes this product inherently adversarial in a real, documented way.

## Status Quo

Four incumbent product buckets, none of which solve this:

1. **Benefits navigation (HealthJoy, Nayya):** Employer-sponsored, focused on helping you pick the right plan and dispute claims you already know about. Not proactive. Not consumer-direct.

2. **HSA/FSA tools and receipt scanners:** Single-domain (only tax-advantaged health accounts). Reactive — you have to know to submit.

3. **Credit card rewards apps:** Single-domain (credit cards only). Some proactive alerts on rewards, no cross-account intelligence.

4. **Nothing:** The most common approach. Google when you remember. Miss when you don't.

**The gap:** The "proactive TurboTax of benefits" — a product that tells you what you're owed and when the window closes — does not exist.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**The user:** 38-year-old single woman, ADD, newly successful. Multiple benefit types (health, accident, supplemental, dental, vision, 401k employer match, credit card perks, wellness reimbursements). Attention span for benefit documentation: 5 minutes max. Never internalizes the details enough to connect them to real life events.

**The motivation split:** 50% financial recapture, 50% principle. Quote: "my generation had to fight to find living wages... taking money from theirs." The adversarial frame is genuine — it drives willingness to engage even with a new product.

**Narrowest wedge:** User uploads their benefit documents (policy PDFs, employer benefits handbook). They describe a life event or purchase in natural language — "I fractured my finger," "I joined a gym," "I'm getting an eye exam tomorrow." The agent responds with personalized, document-grounded guidance: what they're entitled to, how to claim it, whether there's a deadline.

**Trust escalation ladder:**
- Level 1 (wedge): Policy document upload. Low sensitivity. Generic docs, not personal data. Entry point.
- Level 2 (proactive): Optional bank feed, email, calendar access. High value, higher trust ask. Unlocks fully proactive nudges: "looks like you're at urgent care — your accident insurance pays $500 if a fracture is confirmed."

## Constraints

- Consumer-direct (not employer-mediated). No B2B sales cycle.
- Pre-product. No users, no code.
- Founder is the target user — important that the product feels natural to someone with ADD and low patience for dashboards.
- No regulatory expertise yet. Should avoid anything that could be construed as insurance advice that crosses into regulated territory — phrase as "here's what your policy says" not "you should file a claim."

## Premises

1. **Timing is the primary bottleneck, not claims complexity.** The problem is not that filing is too hard — it's that awareness comes after the window closes.
2. **Consumer-direct, not employer-mediated.** The user pays for this, not their HR department.
3. **Adversarial brand is a feature.** Anger at insurance companies drives willingness to upload documents and engage. Trust scales with product experience, not brand softening. In 2026, people hand over everything to their phones — policy PDFs are not particularly sensitive.
4. **Wedge is document-first, integrations second.** Start with low-trust document upload; earn the right to ask for bank/email/calendar access through demonstrated value.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Conversational Benefits Advisor (Selected)
Document upload + conversational interface. AI extracts all entitlements, deadlines, copays, and eligibility criteria from policy PDFs. User describes life events in natural language; agent responds with document-grounded guidance. No integrations, no proactive data access.

Effort: M (6-10 weeks, 1 engineer). Risk: Low.

### Approach B: Life Event Intelligence Platform
Full proactive layer — document ingestion + optional Plaid/email/calendar integration. AI monitors data streams and nudges when qualifying events are detected. The full vision.

Effort: XL (6-18 months, team of 3-4). Risk: High.

### Approach C: Annual Benefits Audit (TurboTax Model)
Once-a-year "benefits tax return" at open enrollment. High WTP, viral "total missed money" mechanic, simpler trust ask. Misses mid-year timing events.

Effort: M (8-12 weeks). Risk: Medium.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach A — Conversational Advisor.**

Ships in 6-10 weeks at prototype fidelity (no auth hardening, no production error handling, no mobile). Tests the single hardest hypothesis: does AI-powered life event → entitlement matching work reliably on real benefit documents? That question must be answered before building the complex integration layer.

**Architecture decision — deadline engine + conversational interface, in that order:**
The subagent correctly identified the core primitive: reliable deadline extraction from unstructured policy text. The founder correctly identified the delivery layer: conversational, not dashboard. These are not competing architectures. The deadline engine is the backend; the conversational interface is the frontend. V1 builds both at prototype depth.

**The matching pipeline (explicit):**
1. PDF ingestion → LLM structured extraction → JSON per benefit: `{name, amount, eligibility_trigger, deadline_type, deadline_value, required_action, source_page, snapshot_category}`
   - `deadline_type` values: `relative` ("within 30 days of event"), `absolute` ("December 31"), `none` (e.g., lifetime maximums, ongoing wellness reimbursements)
   - `eligibility_trigger` may be compound (e.g., "hospital indemnity pays $200/day after day 3"): store as a plain-language string, surface as-is — v1 does not attempt to evaluate compound conditions programmatically
   - Benefits the model cannot confidently extract (null fields, contradictory text, scanned footnotes) are flagged as `needs_review: true` and surfaced to the user as "we couldn't read this section clearly — check page X yourself"
   - `snapshot_category` is assigned by the LLM at extraction time: one of `free` (no action needed to access), `discount` (reduces cost of something user already pays), or `claim` (pays out but requires filing). This field drives the Benefits Snapshot grouping. If ambiguous, default to `claim` — it's better to surface something as requiring action than to let it disappear into "free."
1a. Extracted JSON → **Benefits Snapshot** — plain-language summary grouped by `snapshot_category`, rendered before the user sees any raw table. This is the first screen after upload completes.
2. User describes a life event in natural language
3. LLM semantic match: life event description → relevant benefit records from the extracted JSON
4. Response cites the matched benefit with source document reference ("Your accident policy, page 4")
5. Deadline computation:
   - If `deadline_type = absolute`: use the stated date directly
   - If `deadline_type = relative`: prompt the user for the event date in natural language, normalize to an absolute date, compute deadline. **If event date is ambiguous or not stated, confirm with user before computing or scheduling a reminder** — wrong dates drive financial-stakes reminders
   - If `deadline_type = none`: no deadline displayed; benefit is surfaced as ongoing
6. Optional email reminder at computed deadline (T-30, T-14, T-3 days)

**Failure handling for document parsing (v1):**
- Low-confidence extractions (LLM returns null or "unclear" for a field) surface as "we couldn't read this section clearly — check page X yourself"
- Extracted benefits are displayed in an editable table after upload: columns for benefit name, amount, eligibility trigger, deadline. Users can modify any field or add rows for benefits the model missed entirely. This correction flow is the primary fallback for extraction errors and is load-bearing for the 85% accuracy success criterion.
- No automated claim filing in v1 — agent guides, user acts. This sidesteps the regulated-advice boundary.

**Regulatory boundary (explicit):**
The product says "here's what your policy says and what you need to do to file." It does not say "you should file." The response template is: "Your [policy name] includes a [benefit name] of [amount] for [trigger]. To claim it, you [action]. The deadline is [date]." This is document translation, not insurance advice. Legal review needed before scale.

**Document storage (v1):**
PDFs stored in S3 with user-scoped access. Retention: user-controlled deletion, 12-month auto-expiry with renewal prompt. No sharing between users. Stated clearly in onboarding.

**Session model (v1):**
Email-based auth (magic link). No passwords. User identity is an email + session token. All uploaded documents and extracted benefits are scoped to that identity.

**Core user flow (v1):**
1. Enter email → magic link → land in app
2. Upload benefit documents (drag-and-drop PDF — health, accident, supplemental, employer handbook)
3. Extraction runs silently. When complete, user lands on the **Benefits Snapshot** (Step 4 below) — not a raw table.
4. **Benefits Snapshot (the aha moment):** Immediately after ingestion, Benny presents a plain-language summary organized into three sections:
   - **What you get for free** — zero-cost entitlements requiring no claim: telehealth visits, preventive care at $0 copay, EAP sessions, gym reimbursement that auto-applies, etc. "You already have this. You just have to use it."
   - **Discounts you're entitled to** — reduced costs on things the user already pays for: prescription pricing tiers, preferred provider discounts, vision/dental negotiated rates, credit card price protection. "You're probably overpaying for these."
   - **Coverage that pays out — but only if you file** — benefits that require a claim to collect: accident/hospital indemnity payouts, FSA/HSA reimbursements, supplemental insurance triggers, travel insurance claims. "These are the ones people miss. We're watching them."
   
   This is the trust-building moment: the user sees that Benny has actually read and understood their documents — not just ingested them. The summary is written in plain English, not policy language. Each item links to the source page. The user can act on any item immediately from this screen (guided action, not form filing). This page is also shareable — "look what I didn't know I had" — which is v1's primary organic growth mechanic.

5. **Editable review:** A "review and correct" affordance is accessible from the Snapshot but not the first thing shown. Users who want to verify or add missed benefits can open the full extracted table, edit fields, and add rows. Not load-bearing for the aha moment.
6. **On-demand:** "Tell me what's happening" — user-initiated conversation box. Agent responds with document-grounded guidance: cites policy, states amount, states what to do, states deadline if applicable.
7. **Proactive (with notification opt-in):** Weekly or monthly check-in initiated by the app. Push notification or email: "Quick check-in — what happened this week? Any purchases, injuries, doctor visits, or lifestyle changes?" Conversational prompt surfaces relevant entitlements the user wouldn't have thought to ask about. This is the core timing fix: the app initiates the conversation, not the user. User self-reports; no data integrations required.
8. Optional: "Remind me before the deadline" → email at T-30, T-14, T-3 days

**Out of scope for v1 (deliberate deferrals):**
- Level 2 integrations (Plaid, email, calendar, location) — earnable after trust is established
- Shareable "receipt" social mechanic — v2 growth feature once the core flow is proven
- Mobile app — web only for v1
- Claim preparation or form filing — guide only; user acts
- Multi-policy coordination of benefits logic — flag overlap, do not resolve

## Open Questions

- **Regulatory boundary:** Where does "here's what your policy says" become "insurance advice" in a regulated sense? Legal review needed before scale.
- **Document freshness:** How does the product handle expired policies, mid-year plan changes, open enrollment switches? V1 may need a simple "when did you last upload?" reminder.
- **Claim preparation assistance:** Does v1 generate the actual claim form, or just tell the user what to do? The latter is safer legally; the former is higher value.
- **Pricing model:** Subscription vs. percentage-of-claims-recovered vs. one-time audit? TurboTax charges per return; subscription is stickier.
- **Distribution channel:** Organic (the "I just got $500 back" viral story), paid (Meta/TikTok ads targeting people who recently had a life event), or partnership (financial advisors, employee benefits consultants)?

## Success Criteria

- 100 users upload at least one policy document within 60 days of launch
- 30% of active users have a conversation describing a real life event within their first week (measured via conversation session logs)
- At least 10 users self-report successfully identifying and filing a benefit they would have missed (tracked via in-app "I filed this claim" confirmation flow — user-reported, not automated)
- Extraction quality: human-reviewed sample of 50 extracted benefits shows ≥ 85% accuracy on amount and deadline fields
- Net Promoter Score > 50 surveyed at 30-day mark

## Distribution Plan

**V1 distribution:** Waitlist landing page. Founder tells her own story — the $500 concussion benefit, the anger, the principle. Every early user gets a personal onboarding call. The first 100 users are recruited via the founder's network and targeted social posts describing real benefit misses. No paid acquisition until the core flow is proven.

**Infrastructure:** Web app (Next.js or similar; no mobile for v1). PDF parsing via Claude claude-sonnet-4-6 or GPT-4o structured outputs — model choice to be validated against real extraction quality on a 20-document test corpus before committing. Postgres for user/document/benefits/deadline tables. Cron job for deadline email reminders (daily check: all deadlines within 30/14/3 days → send Resend email). S3 for PDF storage.

## Dependencies

- LLM API access with structured output support (GPT-4o or Claude — validate against real documents before committing)
- Legal review on regulatory boundary for insurance-adjacent products (blocks launch, not build)
- Test corpus of real benefit documents: 20+ documents across benefit types to validate extraction before shipping. **This is the critical path item — it cannot be parallelized with core development.**
- S3 or equivalent for document storage
- Resend (or similar) for transactional email
- No mobile app for v1 — web only

**Timeline note:** The 6-10 week estimate assumes prototype fidelity (functional but not hardened) and that the test corpus and legal review begin concurrently with development. Legal review in particular may gate launch even if the product is code-complete.
